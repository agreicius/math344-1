Now we show that there is such a topology. Note first that the collection \(\operatorname{Top}\) is nonempty, since \(\mathcal{P}(X)\) is a topology (the discrete topology) containing \(\mathcal{A}\text{,}\) and thus the intersection
\begin{equation} \mathcal{T}=\bigcap_{\mathcal{T}'\in \operatorname{Top}}\mathcal{T}'\tag{1.2.6} \end{equation}
is nonempty. Now, by simple properties of intersection it follows that \(\mathcal{T}\) satisfies \(\mathcal{T}\subseteq \mathcal{T}'\) for any topology \(\mathcal{T}'\) containing \(\mathcal{A}\text{.}\) Furthermore, since \(\mathcal{A}\) is contained in \(\mathcal{T}'\) for all \(\mathcal{T}'\in \operatorname{Top}\text{,}\) it is also contained in \(\mathcal{T}\text{,}\) the intersection of the \(\mathcal{T}'\text{.}\) Thus the proof is complete if we can show that \(\mathcal{T}\) is in fact a topology. This in turn follows from the general fact that an intersection of topologies is a topology, the proof of which is left as an exercise.
