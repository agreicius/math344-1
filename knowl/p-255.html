Let \((X,d)\) be a metric space. Given \(x\in X\) and \(\epsilon> 0\) the \(\epsilon\)-ball centered at \(x\) is the subset \(B_\epsilon(x)\subseteq X\) defined as
\begin{equation*} B_\epsilon(x)=\{y\in X\colon d(x,y)< \epsilon\}\text{.} \end{equation*}
In other words, \(B_\epsilon(x)\) is the set of all elements of \(X\) lying within a distance of \(\epsilon\) of \(x\text{.}\) We call a subset \(U\subseteq X\) a metric ball if \(U=B_\epsilon(x)\) for some \(x\in X\) and \(\epsilon> 0\text{.}\)
